Overview

In this homework, we built a real-time cloth simulator from the ground up. We started by constructing the cloth as a grid of point masses connected by structural, shearing, and bending springs, then brought it to life using Verlet integration with Hooke's law forces, damping, and deformation constraints. We added collision handling for both external objects (spheres and planes) and self-collisions, using spatial hashing to keep the self-collision checks efficient. Finally, we implemented several GLSL shaders — Blinn-Phong, texture mapping, bump and displacement mapping, and environment-mapped mirrors — to render the cloth with realistic lighting and materials.

One of the most interesting takeaways was seeing how a small set of physical parameters (spring constant, density, damping) can produce such a wide range of cloth behaviors, from stiff cardboard-like sheets to heavy, drapey fabric. It was also illuminating to compare bump mapping and displacement mapping side by side: bump mapping fakes surface detail convincingly in shading, but displacement mapping actually reshapes the geometry, which becomes especially apparent at the silhouette. Overall, the project tied together physics simulation, spatial data structures, and GPU-based rendering into a cohesive system.

Part 1: Masses and springs

In this part, we implemented the Cloth::buildGrid function to construct a grid of point masses connected by springs. The cloth is represented as a num_width_points × num_height_points grid of point masses spanning the given width and height. For a horizontal orientation, all masses share a y-coordinate of 1 with positions varying across the xz-plane; for a vertical orientation, positions vary across the xy-plane with a tiny random z-offset between -1/1000 and 1/1000. Masses whose indices appear in the pinned vector are marked as pinned so they remain fixed during simulation.

After placing all the point masses (stored in row-major order), we connected them with three types of springs. Structural springs link each mass to its immediate left neighbor and the mass directly above it, enforcing the cloth's basic shape. Shearing springs connect each mass to its upper-left and upper-right diagonal neighbors, preventing the cloth from shearing. Bending springs skip one mass, connecting each mass to the one two positions to its left and two positions above, giving the cloth resistance to folding. For all spring types, we checked boundary conditions to avoid out-of-range accesses.

Part 2: Simulation via numerical integration

In this part, we implemented the Cloth::simulate function, which advances the cloth simulation by one timestep. This involves three steps: computing forces, integrating positions, and constraining spring lengths.

First, we accumulate forces on each point mass. External forces (like gravity) are applied uniformly via F = ma. Then for each spring, we compute the spring correction force using Hooke's law: \( F_s = k_s \cdot (||p_a - p_b|| - l) \), applied in opposite directions to the two endpoint masses. Bending springs use a smaller multiplier than structural and shearing springs to keep them weaker.

Next, we use Verlet integration to update positions. For each unpinned point mass, the new position is computed as \( x_{t+dt} = x_t + (1 - d) \cdot (x_t - x_{t-dt}) + a_t \cdot dt^2 \), where \( d \) is the damping factor. Forces are reset after each integration step to prepare for the next timestep.

Finally, we apply the Provot deformation constraint: if any spring stretches beyond 110% of its rest length, we correct the endpoint positions to bring it back to that limit, splitting the correction equally unless one mass is pinned, in which case the full correction is applied to the unpinned mass.

Parameter Experiments

Default values: ks = 5000, density = 15, damping = 0.2%. We varied one parameter at a time while keeping the others at default.

Spring constant (ks)

With a very low ks (0.001), the cloth has virtually no internal spring forces and sags extremely deeply between the pinned points, stretching out with many fine wrinkles and folds. With a very high ks (500000), the cloth is extremely stiff and barely deforms at all, holding a nearly flat shape with only the slightest curve near the top edge.

Density

With a very low density (0.1 g/cm²), the cloth is nearly weightless and barely sags under gravity, settling almost flat with minimal droop. With a very high density (500 g/cm²), the cloth is extremely heavy and droops deeply under its own weight, producing a shape similar to the low-ks case since the gravitational force far exceeds the spring restoring forces.

Damping

With zero damping (0.00%), the cloth swings back and forth wildly and takes a very long time to settle, as no energy is lost to friction or heat. With full damping (1.00%), the cloth descends extremely slowly and smoothly with no oscillation whatsoever, as if moving through a thick fluid.

Part 3: Handling collisions with other objects

In this part, we implemented collision handling for spheres and planes. For sphere collisions, we check if a point mass is inside the sphere by comparing its distance from the origin to the radius. If so, we compute the tangent point on the sphere's surface, then correct the point mass's position by applying the correction vector from its last position to the tangent point, scaled by (1 - friction).

For plane collisions, we check if a point mass has crossed from one side of the plane to the other since the last timestep. If it has, we compute where it would have intersected the plane, then bump it back to slightly above that point (using a small surface offset) on the side it came from, again scaling the correction by (1 - friction).

Part 4: Handling self-collisions

In this part, we implemented spatial hashing to efficiently detect and handle cloth self-collisions, avoiding the O(n²) cost of checking all point mass pairs.

In hash_position, we partition 3D space into boxes of dimension w × h × t, where w = 3 * width / num_width_points, h = 3 * height / num_height_points, and t = max(w, h). We truncate a point mass's position to its enclosing box and compute a unique float key from those truncated coordinates.

In build_spatial_map, we loop over all point masses, hash their positions, and populate a map from each hash key to a vector of point masses occupying that box.

In self_collide, for a given point mass we look up all candidates sharing the same hash bucket. For each candidate (excluding itself), if the distance between them is less than 2 * thickness, we compute a correction vector that would push them apart to exactly 2 * thickness. The final correction applied is the average of all pairwise corrections, scaled down by simulation_steps.

Part 5: Shaders

A shader program is an isolated program that runs in parallel on the GPU, executing sections of the graphics pipeline. Vertex shaders and fragment shaders work together: the vertex shader transforms vertex attributes (positions, normals) from model space into screen space, writing outputs as varyings. The fragment shader then receives these interpolated varyings and computes the final color of each pixel. By combining these two stages, we can efficiently create lighting and material effects in real time.

Blinn-Phong Shading

The Blinn-Phong model computes lighting as the sum of three components: an ambient term (\( k_a I_a \)) providing a base level of illumination, a diffuse term (\( k_d (I/r^2) \max(0, n \cdot l) \)) for matte surface shading based on the angle of incoming light, and a specular term (\( k_s (I/r^2) \max(0, n \cdot h)^p \)) for shiny highlights based on the half-vector between the light and view directions.

Bump Mapping and Displacement Mapping

Bump mapping modifies only the surface normals based on a height map, creating the illusion of detail without changing the actual geometry. Displacement mapping goes further by also modifying vertex positions according to the height map, producing real geometric deformation. Comparing the two: bump mapping produces convincing surface detail in the shading but the silhouette remains smooth since the geometry is unchanged. Displacement mapping actually deforms the vertices, so the outline becomes bumpy. With a coarse mesh (-o 16 -a 16), displacement mapping produces blocky, jagged results since there aren't enough vertices to capture fine detail, while bump mapping still looks smooth. With a fine mesh (-o 128 -a 128), displacement mapping produces much smoother, more detailed deformation that closely matches the bump-mapped appearance.
